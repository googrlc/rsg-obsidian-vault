While the market is free to set its own pricing, it isn’t necessarily free to set up a dual pricing system that applies to different sets of clients. Dual pricing is the situation in which one policyholder is charged a different rate than another policyholder for the same coverage. Dual Pricing isn’t necessarily unethical; many auto insurance policies, health insurance policies, and property policies have very different prices based on factors such as a person’s driving record, the health and lifestyle of the applicant, and the location of the property.

Dual pricing may be unethical or illegal if insurers or agents charge prices that are based on age, gender, ethnic background, or the length of time the policyholder has been a client. For instance, Claire has two clients, Eric and Alex, both of whom are 28 years old and applying for the same automobile coverages on an identical model of a new car. Eric’s premium is $150 less than Alex’s because Eric has been Claire’s customer for 5 years while Alex is a new customer.
