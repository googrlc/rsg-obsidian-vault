People come by their ethical decisions through various filters. How one approaches the issue can play a critical role in what decision is finally reached. For some people, decisions are situation-specific, but others may think that existing rules and laws are the best deciding factor. Still others believe that decisions that prioritize with the welfare of others will result in the best outcome. Some people make decisions based on duty.

A synergy of these methods is often better than following one way strictly. Because ethical dilemmas vary greatly due to a number of extenuating factors, there is rarely a stock answer that serves all ethical decisions. Those factors may include the environment in which the agent works. If there is little regard for maintaining high ethical standards, it will be difficult for agents to come to the best ethical decisions and be supported for making these choices.

Yet, even when agencies support their agents and promote an atmosphere of high ethical standards, there are often missteps agents make that may not be intentional. One poor choice or inattention to detail could result in unlawful behavior on the agent’s part and could create a situation in which the agent is facing criminal charges.
