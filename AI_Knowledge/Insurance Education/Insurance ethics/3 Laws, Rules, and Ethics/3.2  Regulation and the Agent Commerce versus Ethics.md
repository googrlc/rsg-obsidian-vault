Prior to 1945, several major lawsuits were filed regarding the regulation of the insurance industry, specifically whether it was controlled by the federal government or by the individual states. Congress resolved the matter when it passed the McCarran-Ferguson Act of 1945. The act provided that certain federal antitrust acts apply to the business of insurance only “to the extent that such business is not regulated by state law.” It retained the independent systems of regulation that had been developed by the 50 state governments, thereby preserving their role as the primary regulating authorities for the interstate insurance industry. Today, insurance is regulated by the individual insurance departments in each of the 50 states. Like insurance, ethics is largely regulated by the states. States insurance departments are responsible for the ethical behavior of the insurance agents doing business within their borders. Accordingly, states have established guidelines of expected behavior and in some cases have adopted model laws created by the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC). The NAIC is a trade association of state insurance commissioners that issues model insurance acts that states can adopt.
