The law of agency creates duties and liabilities for agents and insurers.

### Agent Responsibilities

Agents have responsibilities to clients and the insurers they represent.

#### Liability to the Client

Agents have a duty to act within the authority granted to them by the insurer. If the agent exceeds that authority, they may be directly liable to the client. For example, if Joe tells the client that he can provide coverage through a specific insurer that he does not represent, he becomes liable for that action. Joe has acted illegally and unethically. When the agent acts outside their authority, express or otherwise, they (not the insurer) are liable for the consequences.

#### Liability to the Insurer (Principal)

An agent owes the principal a fiduciary duty to act in the best interests of the principal. An agent cannot use the powers the principal has delegated to them to benefit themself at the expense of their principal. For example, suppose that Joe represents ABC insurance Company and collects a $500 premium payment from John, a client. Joes is legally and ethically obligated to forward the payment to ABC Insurance.

### Insurer Responsibilities

Under the law of agency, insurers have responsibilities to clients and to agents.

#### Liability to the Client

The insurer is liable for the agent’s actions when the agent is acting within the scope of the agency-insurer agreement. For example, if the agent is authorized to collect premiums and accepts a late payment from a client, the insurer may be obligated to accept the payment and reinstate the client’s coverage.

#### Liability to the Agent

So long at the agent has acted with authority that has been granted to them, the insurer is liable to pay the agent for duties performed under the law of agency relationship.

### Agent’s Duty to Principal

Once is the agents and the insurer have an established relationship, agents must perform the following specific duties.

- Act on behalf of the insurer and allow the insurer to have oversight of those actions
- Undertake all functions outlined in the contract that the insurer has authorized agent the authority to perform
- Perform duties with the utmost care and due diligence
- Avoid situations that could create a conflict of interest detrimental to the insurer while benefitting the agent
- Keep accurate financial records in accordance with standard business practices
- Collect and remit without delay all funds collected while acting on the insurer’s behalf

An agent cannot behave in a manner whereby the agent is profiting unjustly from the agency relationship. For example, an agent should not redirect business that would have gone to the insurer to another agent or insurer in exchange for monetary gain. Such actions violate the agent’s duty to the insurer and are unethical.

### Terminating an Agent’s Authority

Agency relationships don’t last forever. The following outline some circumstances in which the relationship between the agent and the insurer must be terminated.

#### Agent Withdrawal

Should the agent decide not to work with a particular insurer again, they can withdraw, or terminate, the agency relationship. Most states require this termination to be done in writing and copies sent to both the insurer and the insurance commission.

#### Completed Agency

In cases where the agent and insurer are conducting business on a limited basis, such as an agent purchasing hard-to-find coverage from a wholesale broker representing an insurer, the agency will be a short-term relationship. Both parties usually agree to the limited arrangement in order to facilitate the sale of the product to the customer. The agency relationship would terminate once the business goals have been completed.

#### Insurer Insolvency

Agents are not responsible for representing insurer interests if the insurer has become insolvent and unable to fulfill its obligations to pay agents. However, not all insolvencies automatically void an agency relationship. In cases of reorganization, it could be that the insurer, still in control of its day-to-day business, would still conduct business as usual. In cases where the law has garnered control of the insurer’s business, agents would be released from the agency relationship. State laws will dictate the relationship and termination more exactly.

#### Agency Revocation

Because agency relationships must be mutual, insurers have the right to terminate the relationship, as well. Agents must be notified in writing, and the insurance commission must also receive notification of the termination. Insurers can terminate for various reasons, including a change in product focus, a dissatisfaction with the agent, illegal activity by the agent, insanity, death, or insolvency on the part of either party.

#### Mutual Agreement

In some cases, agency is terminated when both parties agree to it. For example, if the insurer, which has offered life insurance products that the agent is licensed to sell, switches its product focus to commercial property insurance, the agent would likely have little interest in continuing to work with the insurer, and the insurer would see no reason to continue working with an agent who is not focusing in that new product area.

#### Time Expiration

Insurers and agents can set time limits on their relationships. For example, ABC Company is trying to increase business in a particular city. They establish an agreement with agent Marisa to sell their products to her customers for 2 years. Once those 2 years are completed, the agency relationship automatically expires. ABC can choose to extend the relationship in writing, but both parties must agree, and if nothing is done formally, the agency relationship is terminated.
