Business has evolved. Where there used to be a singular focus on stockholder value, there is now a broader focus on the impact of the business operations on others. Business now manages from an ethical perspective. One would be hard pressed to find a business of any significant size operating without a code of ethics in place.

Mission statements, value statements, and vision statements all reflect a company’s ethical approach. For example, Microsoft has its value statement as part of the Microsoft Standards of Business Conduct￼ posted on the company website. Part of that value statement states the following.

![man in suit in the office smiling and holding a tablet](https://www.webce.com/ImageAssets/Graphics/AdobeStock_254678645.jpg?v=638780758800000000)

**Microsoft Values**

Our values are the enduring principles that guide us to do business with integrity as we strive to win trust every day. Our culture is our operating framework—who we are and how we behave. Microsoft cites respect, integrity, and accountability as its primary values. These are both values and ethical constructs. As mentioned in Chapter 1, ethics is considered “a set of moral principles: a theory or system of moral values.”

Still, there are companies and corporate cultures that do not support a healthy ethical environment. Often, management lacks an understanding as to how ethics fit into a business setting. Some of the more prevalent misconceptions include notions such as the following.

- Ethics cannot be managed.
- Compliance equals ethics.
- Ethics come from the employees.
- Ethics are not connected to business and the workplace.

### Myth: Ethics Cannot Be Managed

All one needs to do is look at current history to see the ramifications of a company that does not manage its ethical behavior. From Enron to Wells Fargo, the last 20 years alone have been witness to countless scandals involving falsifying documents, accounting fraud, and obtaining funds illegally. Ethics not only can be managed but must be.

In order to manage an ethical culture and policies, successfully companies and employees must understand how ethics fit into every piece of the business operations. Plans and growth initiatives can sometimes directly conflict with corporate ethics, as was the case with Enron. Growth strategies were given the priority, and the board voted three times to make exceptions to the code of ethics, in fact sacrificing an ethical (and legal) practice for profits.

Despite ethical dilemmas being difficult to unravel at times, companies having a strong code of ethics and an active policy of adherence can help employees know how to come to the right decisions, both for the companies themselves and from a legal standpoint.

### Myth: Compliance Equals Ethics

Just because a company is compliant does not mean the company is acting ethically. For example, if Jessica adds extra personal expenses to her trip reimbursement form, she may not be breaking a law per se, but she is acting unethically. Likewise, if Fred tells his employer that Joanna, a coworker he doesn’t like, is lazy and unproductive, he is not breaking the law, but his spreading rumors about Joanna, even if they were true, is unethical.

While these acts are disturbing because of their devious nature, they can also be a precursor to illegal activity. For example, if Jessica, having gotten away with those extra expenses, then decides to file a workers compensation claim for an injury she sustained on a camping trip, that is now fraud. The same goes for Fred, who may decide to plant stolen property in Joanna’s desk in order to get her fired.

### Myth: Ethics Come from the Employees

Some companies believe their employees are inherently ethical and allow them to control the ethical environment in the business.

As illustrated in previous chapters, people do not hold the same ethical values. Therefore, employees may apply conflicting solutions to the same ethical dilemma.

For example, suppose Ellen, James, and Paul are each asked to determine if the company should drop its email monitoring policy. Ellen, who uses email frequently, thinks the monitoring is intrusive and should be eliminated. James thinks too many workers use email for personal business and the monitoring should be increased. Paul, on the other hand, uses the phone only. He finds email confusing and a cold, impersonal way to contact clients. He thinks the company should eliminate email altogether.

This example demonstrates why businesses should not rely on employees to be the ethical foundation of the organization. As problems become more complex, the clear lines between right and wrong choices blur. It is also where most ethical differences become huge crevasses.

### Myth: Ethics Are Not Connected to Business

As with the example from the Microsoft website, ethics are indeed connected to business and the workplace. Employee manuals convey the company’s expectations from both a productivity and an ethical perspective. For example, an employee manual might restrict employees’ use of corporate intellectual property. Theft of such information is barred by law and ethics.

Workplace behaviors are guided through various policies, values statements, mission statements, and codes of conduct or ethics. Companies must maintain compliance with all applicable laws and regulations. They enact rules to guide, and enforce, responsibility and accountability. By creating expectations around employee actions and behavior, companies are creating ethical boundaries, either consciously or not.
