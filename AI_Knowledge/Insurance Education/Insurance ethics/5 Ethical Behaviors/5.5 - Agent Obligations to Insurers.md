Much like traditional employer-employee relationships, agents are obliged to follow certain rules, regulations, and codes of conduct. When an agent signs a contract with an insurance company to sell its products, both the agent and the insurer have entered into an agreement whereby each party will assume some obligation to the other. For agents, that obligation is known as a _fiduciary_ responsibility. Besides the responsibilities that come with the law of agency, agents signing a contract with an insurer also agree to do the following.

- Follow insurance company procedures
- Exercise loyalty
- Avoid conflicts of interest
- Perform with competency and care
- Conduct business transactions responsibly
- Maintain full disclosure
- Handle responsibly all premium monies

### Follow Insurer Procedures

Insurers have a right to expect that any agent selling their products conduct themselves in a way that reflects the company’s image. Because agents are often independent contractors, insurance companies need assurance that the actions of those acting on their behalf do not result in legal action or in loss of business.

For those reasons, insurers establish their own set of rules around how agents can solicit business and what can or cannot appear in client communication, including advertising. Agents must obey these company directives. Failure to do so constitutes a breach of contract, and the agent then becomes liable for any loss the insurer suffers as a result of the breach.

However, agents are not obligated to follow an insurer’s directive if it would mean committing a criminal act. For example, the insurer cannot require Jane to sell her clients policies that far exceed their needs in order to help the insurer improve profits.

### Exercise Loyalty

When agents contract with insurers, they promise to work for the benefit of the insurance company, not for their own personal benefit. Agents cannot act dishonestly or inappropriately while under contract with the insurance company. More importantly, agents cannot capitalize on a business opportunity that rightfully goes to the insurer.

This obligation of loyalty creates a fine line on which agents must walk, for they also have an obligation of loyalty to their clients to find them the most appropriate coverage for their needs. However, reputable insurers understand and support an agent’s duty to both his clients and his insurer.

### Avoid Conflicts of Interest

It has been said that one cannot serve two masters. That is certainly true for the captive agent. (A captive agent represents one insurer or has a contractual obligation to give the company first right of refusal on sales.) Captive agents are prohibited from working simultaneously for two insurers selling competing products. That does not extend into products the insurer does not offer; therefore, a captive agent selling inland marine insurance could also work for a company that sells other types of coverage but does not offer inland marine coverage.

An independent agent must represent both sides of the insurance transaction: the insurer and the client. Agents handle this role without creating a conflict of interest by first representing the client during the product selection process. Once a product has been selected, the agent must then switch loyalty to the insurer for the application, underwriting, and management processes.

### Perform with Competence and Care

In order to work with insurers, agents must possess the skills required for the job. Also, agents are expected to perform their duties with the utmost care. Yet what is meant by “competence”?

In short, agents are expected to be able to conduct business with a similar level of skill held by others practicing in the same job. For example, Rhonda must be licensed to sell insurance and understand how to put together a client presentation and complete an insurance application. Why? Because it is required of all agents to understand the basics of the job.

However, Rhonda is not expected to sell insurance products with which she is unfamiliar, such as commercial errors and omissions policies or kidnap and ransom coverage. Instead, she is able to contact brokers or insurers to help her locate and sell these products.

Should Rhonda not be able to handle even the basics of insurance sales, she could be deemed negligent. Should a loss occur as a result of her negligence, Rhonda could be held liable for any damages to the client and/or the insurer.

### Conduct Business Transactions Responsibly

Agents are required by insurers, and many state laws, to transact business in a timely, responsible manner. Applications and premium payments must be submitted within a short time frame. A late application or payment can cause a client to go uninsured, and if a loss occurs, the insurer may be liable for a claim on an application it has not yet received.

### Maintain Full Disclosure

Agents are required to fully disclose all information from the client that would affect the application’s approval. For example, if Richard knows that Melissa enjoys skydiving as a weekend sport, he must inform the life insurer to which she is making application. The added risks to Melissa’s well-being are a vital piece of information that is needed to make a sound coverage determination.

Not only is the agent obligated to disclose information at the point of application, but agents must also disclose all applicable information at the time of a claim filing. For example, if John has been told by Maurice’s widow that Maurice was depressed for a month before his death, he must inform the insurer. John cannot make judgments, nor should he make assumptions. His role is to pass all information on to the insurance company.

### Handle Premiums Responsibly

Insurers often authorize agents to collect first premium payments. These funds must be held in a trust or escrow account and must not be comingled with any of the agent’s personal or business funds. To properly handle premiums, agents must have a dedicated account for the sole purpose of handling premium funds.

For example, if Jeff hands Ben a check after completing a life insurance application, Ben has a responsibility to place the money in an escrow designated specifically for such premium payments. Ben must redirect the funds to the insurer within a given period of time. He cannot hold the funds indefinitely, nor can he refuse to forward the funds to the insurer once coverage is in place. He must transfer the funds promptly to ensure the policyholder’s interests are served as well as the insurer’s.
