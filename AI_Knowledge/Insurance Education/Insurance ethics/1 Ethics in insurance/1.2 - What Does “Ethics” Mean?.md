Ethics can be difficult to define because it is a broad term that includes various concepts and scenarios. For the insurance agent, it is not as important to know the full scope of ethics as it is to understand how to apply ethics to working relationships.

According to _Merriam-Webster Dictionary_, “ethics” can be defined in the following ways.

1. A set of moral principles**:** a theory or system of moral values
2. The principles of conduct governing an individual or a group
3. The discipline dealing with what is good and bad and with moral duty and obligation

Ethics, by its very nature, is a discipline that takes into account morality and moral principles. For this reason, it is easy to see why many people confuse “ethics” with “morality.” However, there is a distinct difference between the terms. Morality is principles of right and wrong behavior based on a person’s beliefs; ethics is principles of right and wrong behavior established by a group or community. Morality is defined by an individual, and ethics are defined by a group of people or society.

Ethics remains difficult to explain, and is often confused with socially accepted behaviors, laws, or sometimes even religion. While ethics can be associated with any of these areas, none of them are where ethics is founded.

Ethics are how people ought to act in certain situations or with various people. It is a code of behavior that can differ from one community or group to another. However, ethics are not a code of laws. Laws are written by governments, and ethical codes are created by groups, including business organizations. While many laws (such as those prohibiting theft) include ethical principles, legal acts (such as lying to a friend) aren’t necessarily ethical. Ethics are the parameters, developed out of one’s experiences or immediate surroundings, by which one conducts oneself in public settings such as the workplace or in a client’s home.

For example, very few people would drive over someone’s lawn, have a loud conversation during a piano recital, or take things from the supermarket without paying. Society has formed rules based on an ethical structure that dictate how people should behave. Someone who doesn’t value those ethical structures may violate those codes, perhaps by stealing from a grocery store or speaking loudly during a musical performance. Still, most people abide by the rules of ethics that exist within their group so that society can function smoothly.

Morality is a more personal form of ethics—one’s own personal code of conduct. Each person’s morality is unique to them and their situation. For example, Jane may think it is acceptable to patronize restaurants, hair salons, and other businesses without leaving tips. Jane values frugality and believes that tips are an unnecessary expenditure. Beth, on the other hand, puts a high value on fairness. She believes that service workers are underpaid and leaves generous tips when patronizing local businesses. Jane’s morals regarding tipping are clearly different from Beth’s. Yet, neither Jane nor Beth is necessarily wrong, as both are living within their own set of moral standards. Because ethics is a system of morals, ethics and morals are interrelated. For example, Jane may teach her children not to take things from their siblings’ rooms without asking because, in her mind, that’s stealing. That moral lesson Jane is teaching becomes an ethical code, as well as a law, when applied outside her home. Taking other people’s things without asking is considered stealing, according to the ethical code in many societies.

### Ethics in Business

The role of ethics and morality also play a part in the business world. Because morality varies so widely among individuals, ethics become an important component to conducting business. For business, a set of expectations and rules that all employees must follow is vital to the company’s reputation and position both in the business community and with the public.

A business code of ethics outlines what behaviors the firm considers to be right and wrong so that employees understand what is expected. A written code encourages the right, or expected, behaviors and discourages wrong behaviors. Companies develop values, mission statements, employee handbooks, training, and other programs and guidelines to define their ethical position and expectations to the public and to employees.

While developing a companywide ethics code is relatively easy, implementing it is not. If senior management treats the ethics code as simply a procedural document, employees may never embrace it or even know that it exists. If company leaders fail to communicate and enforce the code of ethics across the organization, employees may behave improperly out of ignorance. Some may act unethically because they think management doesn’t care. Even prominent companies can find themselves mired in ethical difficulties. The following examples demonstrate the importance of an ethical structure within a corporate setting.

#### Enron

In 2001, the most famous example of corporate misdeeds—the Enron scandal—was revealed. Layers of financial misstatements, conflicts of interest, unethical practices, and questionable accounting practices had led to the overstatement of earnings and the eventual bankruptcy of the company. According to Bethany McLean and Peter Elkind, authors of _The Smartest Guys in the Room: The Amazing Rise and Scandalous Fall of Enron_, “The Enron scandal grew out of a steady accumulation of habits, values, and actions that began years before and finally spiraled out of control.”

Enron did have a code of ethics, which the company titled “Our Values.” From that code comes the following excerpt.

INTEGRITY: We work with customers and prospects openly, honestly, and sincerely. When we say we will do something, we will do it; when we say we cannot or will not do something, then we won’t do it.

Clearly the company did not abide by its own credo. Instead, in at least one case, a senior officer was granted three separate exemptions from the company’s code of ethics in order to create entities for the company from which that officer would personally profit.

#### Fannie Mae

The Federal National Mortgage Association (Fannie Mae) buys loans from lenders and then packages them into securities (called mortgage-backed securities), which it sells to investors in the secondary mortgage market. Lenders can use the proceeds of their sales to Fannie Mae to make more loans.

In 2006, prior to the housing market crash, the federal government charged Fannie Mae with securities laws violations, including accounting fraud and deceiving investors. The Office of Federal Housing Enterprise Oversight charged Fannie Mae’s executive with receiving huge bonuses by manipulating accounting. The company was fined $400 million, which it paid without admitting or denying fault.

#### AIG

The insurance industry is not immune to scandals involving alleged or actual unethical behavior. In 2004, New York’s then-Attorney General Elliott Spitzer filed a lawsuit against American International Group (AIG), Marsh & McLennan, and several other insurers for allegedly cheating customers. The suit charged that the defendants steered clients to insurers with which they had lucrative payoff agreements and that they would solicit rigged bids for insurance contracts.1

#### Wells Fargo

A more recent financial services example involved Wells Fargo & Company and its subsidiary, Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. Between 2002 and 2016, these organizations pressured employees to meet unrealistic sales goals. This, in turn, led thousands of employees to provide millions of accounts or products to customers under false pretenses or without consent, often by creating false records or misusing customers’ identities.

### Lessons Learned

The cases outlined above demonstrate the importance of a written code of ethics that is supported by senior management, communicated to all employees, and consistently enforced. Enron had an extensive code of ethics (64 pages!) that senior management clearly ignored.2 In the Fannie Mae scandal, the executives were allegedly redirecting funds, with little or no attention to their 16-page code of ethics titled _This Is How I Work_. As for Eliot Spitzer, 4 years after he sued AIG and Marsh & McLennan, Mr. Spitzer resigned as governor of New York. His resignation followed a report by the _New York Times_ that he had been participating in unsavory behavior.3

Wells Fargo ultimately admitted that it had collected millions of dollars in fees and interest to which the company was not entitled, harmed the credit ratings of certain customers, and unlawfully misused customers’ sensitive personal information. In 2020, Wells Fargo and its subsidiary, Wells Fargo Bank, N.A., agreed to pay $3 billion to resolve their potential criminal and civil liability stemming from its unethical practices.

### Ethics in Professional Actions

Every member of the Independent Insurance Agents & Brokers of America, Inc. (IIABA), is expected to follow IIABA’s Code of Ethics4 , which states, in part, the following.

I will do my part to uphold and upbuild the American Agency System which has developed insurance to its present fundamental place in the economic fabric of our nation, and to my fellow members of the Independent Insurance Agents of America, I pledge myself always to support right principles and oppose bad practices in the business.

Other professional associations in the insurance industry have codes of ethics as well. These codes outline the organization’s values and set standards of behavior that members must follow. Because organizations differ in terms of their business models, goals, and expectations, most create their own ethical standards with which agents must comply.

For example, Janet’s company has a code of conduct that requires employees to keep all information regarding the company’s intellectual property confidential. Yet, while giving a presentation at a conference, Janet reveals that her company’s underwriting team is creating a hot new insurance product to insure widgets. She gives conference attendees details of the new product, including policy limits, underwriting challenges, and expected market share. Janet directly violated her company’s code of conduct.
