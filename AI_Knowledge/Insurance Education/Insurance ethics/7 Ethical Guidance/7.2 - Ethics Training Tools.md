To help agents understand the importance of behaving ethically, most states require ethics training. For agents to navigate more complex ethical situations, agencies and insurers should be promoting ongoing training, either formally or informally.

### Formal Training

A formal ethics training program is set up by the company or insurer and is designed with a stated objective and agenda. There is a goal, a learning platform, and often measurable results. Formal training should comprise the following components.

- Current skills and knowledge of the participants
- Gaps in knowledge or skills to be addressed
- Learning goals and evaluation steps
- Measurement of training results

In most organizations, employees and management are already following policies, procedures, and various conduct codes. What formal training does is reinforce and strengthen those rules by helping employees think actively about ethical decision-making.

A good training program will include the following.

- An overview, including example scenarios, of current policies and ethics codes
- A group review of current policies and codes
- Practice sessions where participants are presented an ethical dilemma and instructed to come up with solutions
- Decision-making processes that they can use beyond the training session
- Exercises that create understanding of the employees’ own ethical behaviors

### Informal Training

Arguably, there is no substitute for lessons learned on the job. Informal ethics training is lessons learned from facing particular situations and establishing a method by which to come to resolution. Most of informal training is accomplished on a personal level. Savvy companies and agents can harness those lessons learned and build a knowledge repository by regularly reviewing ethical dilemmas with the team. However, most companies do not have a process in place by which they can capture this knowledge.

Ironically, that kind of review calls for a more formalized process of review, something most companies do not consider. A study conducted by the Chief Learning Officer Business Intelligence Board revealed that 74 percent of learning executives surveyed said they did not track informal learning in their organizations.16

Still, the results of such review meetings can help companies address and possibly reduce the occurrence of ethical malfeasance and help bolster team involvement in adopting an ethical business operation. A number of businesses have begun to put in place knowledge management processes, which serve to capture the knowledge learned and develop ways in which to share this knowledge.

For independent agents, associations can serve as repositories of knowledge. Lessons learned shared through one-to-one connections can often serve the same purpose as the formalized meetings designed to do the same thing. Agents should be actively seeking opportunities to examine situations with colleagues, insurers, even brokers. In most cases, someone has experienced already that same type of dilemma and can offer lessons learned.

### Ethical Dilemma Checklist

For agents lacking immediate access to a sound support system, there are other tools that can help determine the right course of action. Laura L. Nash, senior research fellow at Harvard Business School, has written numerous books on ethics in a business setting. She developed the following 12-question checklist to help managers and agents approach ethical dilemmas in a more systematic way. Her checklist is as follows.

1. Have you defined the problem accurately?
2. How would you define the problem if you stood on the other side of the fence?
3. How did this situation occur in the first place?
4. To whom and to what do you give your loyalty as a person and as a member of the corporation?
5. What is your intention in making this decision?
6. How does this intention compare with the probable results?
7. Whom could your decision or action injure?
8. Can you discuss the problem with the affected parties before you make your decision?
9. Are you confident that your position will be as valid over a long period of time as it seems now?
10. Could you disclose without qualm your decision or action to your boss, your CEO, the board of directors, your family, society as a whole?
11. What is the symbolic potential of your action if understood? if misunderstood?
12. Under what conditions would you allow exceptions to your stand?

### Asking Quality Questions

While Nash’s checklist is a good guideline, there are times when the dilemma requires more examination. When agents are talking with clients or going over advice from other agents or from management or insurers, they should be examining those responses to ensure that there are no hidden agendas.

One approach would be to engage each communicant with what’s known as Socratic questions. The method is a disciplined approach to questioning that is systematic and deep and, in ethical situations, focuses on the issues or problems being addressed. Some examples of Socratic questions17 include the following.

- **Questions for clarification.** Example: Why do you say that? How does this relate?
- **Questions that probe assumptions.** Example: How can we verify or disprove this? What else can we assume instead of this?
- **Questions that probe reasons and evidence.** Example: What would be an example?
- **Questions about viewpoints and perspectives.** Example: What would be an alternative? Why is this best?
- **Questions that probe implications and consequences.** Example: What are you implying? How does this affect…?
- **Questions about the question.** Example: What is the point of this question? What does … mean?

Using questions to understand both motives and possible conflicts can help agents form an ethically sound decision that better complies with all ethical requirements while addressing the issue itself.
