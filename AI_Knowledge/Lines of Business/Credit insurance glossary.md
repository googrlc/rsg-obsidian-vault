## 4 - Glossary

**accounts receivable coverage:** Another name for credit insurance. Also a coverage that insures against loss of sums owed to the insured by its customers that are uncollectible because of damage by an insured peril to accounts receivable records.

**actual premium (in credit insurance):** Premium as computed based on the insured’s actual sales volume for the year.

**aggregate first loss (AFL):** The deductible in a credit insurance policy. Also known as the primary loss amount.

**Atradius:** One of the three biggest credit insurers.

**blanket coverage (in credit insurance):** Provides a small amount of coverage for customers who are not named in the policy and for which no governing agency rating is available.

**business credit insurance:** See credit insurance.

**business risk insurance:** See credit insurance.

**can’t pay:** Financial inability to pay, due, for example, to bankruptcy or some other type of insolvency.

**catastrophic credit insurance policy:** See excess credit insurance policy.

**claims threshold:** The amount below which a loss will not be filed as a claim.

**Coface:** One of the three biggest credit insurers. (Pronounced KO-fahss.)

**coinsurance percentage (in credit insurance policies):** The percentage of each insured loss that is not indemnified by the insurer and that the insured has to bear for its own account. Typically between 10 percent and 20 percent. Also known as the primary loss percentage or insured percentage. Different from coinsurance in property insurance policies.

**collateral:** An asset that can be pledged to secure the money being borrowed.

**commercial businesses:** Businesses that sell to other businesses.

**comprehensive credit insurance:** See portfolio credit insurance.

**credit disability insurance**: Also known as accident and health insurance, coverage that makes payments on the loan if the borrower becomes ill or injured and cannot work**.**

**credit insurance:** Insurance that covers the risk that a customer might not, under certain circumstances, pay a supplier for goods or services sold on credit. Also known as accounts receivable insurance, trade credit insurance, credit risk insurance, business credit insurance, export credit insurance, trade risk insurance, and business risk insurance.

**credit life insurance:** Coverage that pays off all or some of the loan if the borrower dies.

**credit property insurance:** Protects personal property used to secure the loan if it is destroyed by events such as theft, accident, or natural disasters.

**credit risk insurance:** See credit insurance.

**default:** The fact that an invoice has gone unpaid for a certain extended period of time.

**deposit premium:** Premium paid at the beginning of the policy year to initiate coverage. May be a percentage of the initial premium.

**discretionary credit limits (DCL) (in credit insurance):** Provide coverage for credit extended to a customer at the insured’s discretion up to a certain limit.

**80-20 rule:** Eighty percent of a business’s revenue comes from 20 percent of its customers.

**eighty-twenty rule:** See 80-20 rule.

**Euler Hermes:** One of the three biggest credit insurers.

**excess credit insurance policy:** Credit insurance policy under which the insured pays its own customer default losses up to a certain high level, after which point benefits may begin to be paid from the insurer. Also known as catastrophic credit insurance policy.

**export credit insurance:** See credit insurance.

**factoring:** The purchase of a business’s accounts receivables by the insurer.

**gross margin:** A percentage that expresses how much gross profit the business realizes for each dollar of sales.

**ground-up credit insurance:** See portfolio credit insurance.

**Incoterms:** A set of terms with standardized meanings for use in global trade developed by the International Chamber of Commerce. Most Incoterms consist of three capital letters.

**initial premium (in credit insurance):** Premium computed based on the insured’s expected sales volume for the coming year.

**insolvency:** The financial condition of being unable to pay one’s bills. Different credit insurers use different definitions of insolvency.

**insured percentage:** See coinsurance percentage (in credit insurance policies).

**investment grade:** A company whose bond issues are rated BBB or higher by Standard & Poor’s or Baa or higher by Moody’s.

**involuntary unemployment insurance:** Also known as involuntary loss of income, makes loan payments if the borrower loses his or her job through no fault of his or her own, such as a layoff.

**key account policies:** Credit insurance policies that cover only an insured’s large accounts. Also known as segmented portfolio policies.

**letter of credit:** A bank’s guarantee that the customer will make timely payment of its bill.

**multidebtor credit insurance:** See portfolio credit insurance.

**nonqualifying loss (NQL):** A loss that falls below the claims threshold. Insureds bear the entire risk for all nonqualifying losses.

**NQL:** See nonqualifying loss.

**open account:** Credit terms allowing payment within a certain time after delivery.

**policy amount (in credit insurance):** A limit on the total amount of money that will be paid for all claims under the policy.

**political risk insurance:** Insurance covering the risk of political events such as trade embargoes or nationalization of industries that may interfere with obtaining payments due from foreign customers.

**portfolio credit insurance:** The standard form of credit insurance designed to cover all of an insured’s accounts. Also known as comprehensive credit insurance, multidebtor credit insurance, whole-turnover credit insurance, and ground-up credit insurance.

**primary loss amount:** See aggregate first loss (AFL).

**primary loss percentage:** See coinsurance percentage (in credit insurance policies).

**product cycle:** See turnover.

**retail businesses:** Businesses that sell items directly to individual members of the public.

**segmented portfolio policies:** See key account policies.

**single buyer policies:** See single risk policies.

**single debtor policies:** See single risk policies.

**single risk policies:** Credit insurance policies written to cover just one account. Also known as single buyer or single debtor policies.

**terminal premium (in credit insurance):** Difference between the deposit premium and the actual premium.

**trade credit insurance:** See credit insurance.

**trade risk insurance:** See credit insurance.

**turnover:** How long it takes for each dollar of invested capital to produce a sale. Also referred to as a product cycle.

**unexpected loss:** The loss that has not been planned for.

**whole-turnover credit insurance:** See portfolio credit insurance.

**won’t pay:** Protracted default; the customer has not paid a valid invoice that is far past due although there is no formal evidence of financial inability to pay.
